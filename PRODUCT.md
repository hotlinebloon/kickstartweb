# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Kickstart serves employers who need to post paid opportunities, review applicants, and monitor intern progress after acceptance. It also serves job seekers, especially young people looking for internships, micro-projects, entry-level roles, and a clearer way to prove their fit than a CV alone. The current prototype is also used by demo evaluators who need to understand the full applicant-to-growth workflow quickly.

## Product Purpose

Kickstart helps employers create job postings, receive structured applications, accept candidates into placements, and track intern work through tasks, review states, feedback, and progress profiles. It helps applicants find opportunities, apply with proof of skill, and keep building visible evidence after they are hired. Success means the product feels credible enough for employers to pay for postings while remaining clear and useful for applicants.

## Brand Personality

Clear. Serious. Growing.

The interface should feel direct, trustworthy, and oriented around development over time. It should communicate professional momentum without becoming cold, cluttered, or childish. The signature visual idea is a whole-product blueprint environment: the web background should feel like a structured matching workspace, while cards and forms stay clean, white, and readable above it.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic SaaS styling, childish colors, swipe-app energy, cluttered dashboards, cold corporate design, and status systems that rely only on color. The product should not feel like a generic job board, social app, school LMS, or heavy HR portal.

## Design Principles

1. Make employer work feel accountable: posting, reviewing, accepting, and monitoring should read as a serious business workflow.
2. Use the blueprint as the web environment: the full app background can carry the structured matching pattern, while active job cards, application cards, and decision panels remain clean white surfaces.
3. Keep both sides oriented: applicants and employers should always know where they are in the opportunity, application, placement, or task lifecycle.
4. Use structured proof over vague claims: applications, reviews, and progress screens should surface concrete evidence and next actions.
5. Preserve clarity under density: dashboards can carry meaningful information, but status, hierarchy, and actions must stay scannable.
6. Keep the product shell white and blue: blue is the main system color for action, navigation, selected states, links, and focus; red is reserved for reject, delete, error, danger, and critical warnings.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA contrast and interaction standards. Support reduced motion. Status indicators must be color-blind-safe by pairing color with text, shape, iconography, or explicit labels. The interface should avoid color-only meaning in application states, task states, review decisions, and progress signals.
